Chapter Summaries

Introduction — Why Research? Intro▼
Core Idea

Lambert opens by distinguishing research from casual curiosity. Research is systematic, purposeful, and rigorous — it follows deliberate procedures designed to produce trustworthy knowledge. The Introduction frames the student project as a learning exercise in the full research process, not a demand for groundbreaking discovery.

Key Quote
"Doing a research project develops skills in planning, carrying out, and reporting an investigation — that is the main goal." — Lambert (2012), Introduction (paraphrased)

Chapter 1 — What Is Research? Ch 1▼
Core Idea

Chapter 1 defines research as systematic inquiry that generates new knowledge or understanding. Lambert distinguishes it from internet browsing by its deliberate, structured approach. It introduces the spectrum of research types.

Types of Research
  • Basic / Pure — builds theoretical knowledge; no immediate practical application
  • Applied — designed to solve a real-world problem
  • Qualitative — explores meaning, experience, interpretation; words and themes
  • Quantitative — measures, counts, tests hypotheses; numbers and statistics
  • Mixed methods — combines both approaches
Characteristics of Good Research
  • Systematic — follows structured, transparent procedures
  • Purposeful — driven by a clear question or aim
  • Rigorous — evidence-based, not anecdotal or opinion-driven
  • Iterative — the research cycle loops back; not always linear

Chapter 2 — Paradigms & Ethics Ch 2▼
What Is a Paradigm?

A paradigm is a worldview — shared beliefs about the nature of reality (ontology) and how we can know it (epistemology). It is the invisible framework shaping every research decision you make.

The Three Main Paradigms
  • Positivism — reality is objective and measurable; knowledge comes from observation; favours quantitative methods; researcher stays neutral
  • Interpretivism — reality is socially constructed and context-dependent; focuses on meaning; favours qualitative methods; researcher acknowledges subjectivity
  • Critical / Transformative — research should challenge power structures and promote social justice; researcher takes an advocacy stance
Core Ethical Principles
  • Informed consent — participants understand the study and agree voluntarily
  • Confidentiality — identities and data are protected; anonymisation used where needed
  • No harm — physical, psychological, and social risks minimised
  • Honesty — data reported accurately; no fabrication or deception
