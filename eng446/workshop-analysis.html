Thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006): Step 1 — Familiarise. Step 2 — Generate initial codes. Step 3 — Search for themes. Step 4 — Review themes. Step 5 — Define and name themes. Step 6 — Write up. A 'code' is a short label for a unit of meaning. A 'theme' is a pattern across codes.
Assign a code to each interview excerpt

These excerpts are from interviews about peer feedback. Choose the most appropriate code from the dropdown for each. Click "Check codes" when done.

From codes to themes — how it works
Example codes from 10 interviews about peer feedback:
Confidence boost · Embarrassment · Feeling judged · Helpful suggestions · Unclear comments · Trust in peers · Preference for teacher feedback · Learning from giving feedback · Time pressure · Lack of training

Grouping into themes:
→ Theme 1: Emotional responses to peer feedback (Confidence boost, Embarrassment, Feeling judged)
→ Theme 2: Perceived quality of feedback (Helpful suggestions, Unclear comments, Preference for teacher feedback)
→ Theme 3: Structural challenges (Time pressure, Lack of training)
→ Theme 4: Learning through giving feedback (Learning from giving feedback, Trust in peers)

Each theme needs at least 3 data points across different participants to be credible.
Reading quantitative data tables: Check N (number of participants), M (mean = average), SD (standard deviation = spread of scores), and any significance values (p). Scores above the scale midpoint indicate agreement; below = disagreement.

How to report descriptive statistics in your findings section
"Participants strongly agreed that peer feedback improves their writing quality (M = 4.12, SD = 0.73) and that they learn from giving feedback to others (M = 4.24, SD = 0.65). However, participants disagreed with preferring peer feedback over teacher feedback (M = 2.31, SD = 1.22), suggesting that while they value peer feedback as a learning tool, they continue to regard teacher feedback as the primary authoritative source. The relatively high SD for this item (1.22) indicates considerable variability in student preferences."
Notice: state the mean → describe what it means → compare items → explain what the SD tells you.
