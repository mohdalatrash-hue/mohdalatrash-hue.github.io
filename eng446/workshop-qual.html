Braun & Clarke (2006), six steps: 1 Familiarise · 2 Generate initial codes · 3 Search for themes · 4 Review themes · 5 Define & name themes · 6 Write up. A code is a short label for one unit of meaning; a theme is a pattern across many codes.

How many data points make a theme?
A theme is not a single striking quote — it is a pattern. A defensible theme is supported by multiple coded extracts, ideally from several different participants. In this study each of the seven themes is illustrated by quotes from distinct learners (P1, P2, P5, P6, P7, P9, P11, P14, P15, P18…), which is what makes the pattern credible rather than anecdotal.
Trustworthiness is the qualitative counterpart of validity and reliability. Watch for: inductive vs deductive coding, saturation, inter-coder reliability, and member checking.

Writing up a theme follows a three-move pattern: (1) state the theme → (2) present evidence (a participant quote) → (3) interpret what it means for your research question.
A worked example — Theme 1 from the real study
Claim: Several learners traced their writing anxiety to a lack of knowledge about the topic itself.
Evidence: As P7 explained, "It's hard to think about things I don't know much about, especially when I don't have enough information or resources to write about them."
Interpretation: For these students, anxiety is not only linguistic — it begins before any English is written, in the absence of content knowledge. This suggests that pre-writing support (building topic knowledge and supplying resources) could reduce anxiety at its source.
