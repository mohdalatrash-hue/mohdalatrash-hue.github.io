A strong research question is specific (not vague), researchable (answerable with data you can collect), and significant (it matters). It usually contains a phenomenon, a context, and a relationship or outcome to investigate.

What makes a GOOD research question?
✓ Strong examples
  • To what extent does social media use correlate with academic achievement among EFL students at Qassim University?
  • How do Saudi undergraduate students perceive the effectiveness of peer feedback in academic writing?
✗ Weak examples
  • Is social media bad? (too vague, binary)
  • What are the effects of everything on student learning? (too broad)
  • Is cheating wrong? (not researchable — ethical claim, not empirical)
Scope refers to how broad or narrow your research is. Too broad = you cannot realistically collect all the data needed. Too narrow = your findings are trivial and ungeneralisable. Good scope: achievable in the time frame, with the participants you have access to, with real findings that matter.

Common scope problems and fixes
Problem: Too many variables
"The effect of social media, peer pressure, family background, and teaching style on student motivation."
Fix: Pick one independent variable. Focus on what your group can measure.
Problem: Entire country as context
"EFL learners in Saudi Arabia" → millions of people.
Fix: "EFL undergraduate students at Qassim University, Department of English" — specific, accessible.
Problem: Too small a context
"The two students in my study group." → findings are trivial.
Fix: Aim for enough participants that patterns emerge (qualitative: 6–15; quantitative: 30+).
