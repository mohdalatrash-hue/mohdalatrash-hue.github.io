Summary vs. synthesis, gap-spotting, structure types, and a peer-review checklist

Summary tells the reader what ONE source says. Synthesis weaves multiple sources together to build a larger argument — showing agreement, disagreement, patterns, or gaps across several studies. A literature review is synthesis, not a chain of summaries.

How to convert a summary into synthesis
✗ Summary (weak)
"Hassan (2019) found that peer feedback improved student writing. Al-Rashidi (2020) also found that peer feedback was useful. Ngo (2021) studied peer feedback and found similar results."
✓ Synthesis (strong)
"Multiple studies confirm that peer feedback positively impacts EFL writing quality (Al-Rashidi, 2020; Hassan, 2019; Ngo, 2021), though the degree of improvement varies by context — Hassan (2019) found stronger gains in revision depth, while Ngo (2021) noted greater improvement in grammatical accuracy."
Key moves in synthesis: group sources by theme, signal agreement/disagreement, show how one study extends another, identify what is still unknown.
The research gap is the missing piece in the existing literature — what hasn't been studied, what population has been overlooked, or what method hasn't been applied. Your research proposal fills this gap. Practice finding gap language in real abstracts.

Common gap-signalling phrases
Limitation gaps
  • "Previous studies have focused only on…"
  • "Little attention has been paid to…"
  • "Most research has been conducted in Western contexts…"
Uncertainty gaps
  • "It remains unclear whether…"
  • "No consensus has been reached on…"
  • "The existing literature has yet to examine…"
Population gaps
  • "Studies involving Arab EFL learners are scarce…"
  • "No studies have examined this phenomenon among…"
Method gaps
  • "Previous work has relied predominantly on…"
  • "A qualitative approach has not been applied to…"

What a thematic lit review paragraph looks like
[Topic sentence — state the theme] Peer feedback has been consistently linked to improvements in EFL writing quality across multiple contexts.
[Evidence — synthesise 2–3 sources] Hassan (2019) and Al-Rashidi (2020) both found significant gains in revision depth, while Ngo (2021) noted improvements in grammatical accuracy among Vietnamese EFL learners.
[Critical comment — agree/extend/contest] However, these studies focused primarily on undergraduate contexts; the effect of peer feedback among secondary students remains less established (Park & Lee, 2022).
[Bridge to gap / your study] This gap motivates the present study, which examines peer feedback effectiveness among EFL learners at Qassim University.
