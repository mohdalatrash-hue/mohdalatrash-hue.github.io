Quantitative Data Analysis

Work with the real statistics from a published EFL experiment — read the tables, interpret a t-test, and turn numbers into findings.

📄 The dataset behind this workshop

Every table below is taken from a real published quasi-experiment: Afifah & Devana (2020), Speaking Skill through Task-Based Learning in an EFL Classroom. Two groups (experimental, taught with Task-Based Learning; control, taught conventionally) sat a pre-test and a post-test. You will analyse the actual results.

Descriptive statistics summarise a dataset. Mean = average. Median = middle value. Mode = most frequent. SD (standard deviation) = how spread out the scores are: a small SD means scores cluster near the mean; a large SD means they are widely scattered.

The paired-samples t-test asks: is the change from pre-test to post-test real, or could it be chance? The key number is Sig. (2-tailed), the p-value. If p < .05, the change is statistically significant (unlikely to be chance). If p > .05, it is not significant.

The trap: a bigger mean is not always a significant one
Both groups improved on average — but only the experimental group's gain is statistically significant (p = .000). The control group also rose by 2.77 points, yet p = .186 means that rise could plausibly be chance. Always check the p-value before claiming an effect — never argue from the mean difference alone.

Reporting Likert data in your findings
"An overwhelming majority of students agreed that Task-Based Learning made English enjoyable, with 31 of 36 (86%) strongly agreeing and the remainder agreeing — not a single negative response (Item 1). Responses to Item 5 were the most divided: 22 students (61%) reported task-related anxiety while 14 (39%) disagreed, suggesting that although learners value the approach, a sizeable minority still find tasks stressful."
Pattern: convert counts → percentages → identify the strongest and the most divided items → interpret what the split means.

What the standard deviation tells you
Two classes can share the same mean of 60 but tell very different stories. A class with scores tightly bunched around 60 has a small SD (consistent performance). A class with some students near 90 and others near 30 also averages 60, but has a large SD (polarised performance). The mean alone hides this — which is why findings always report M and SD together.
