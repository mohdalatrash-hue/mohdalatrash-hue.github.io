Literature Review: Structure & Evaluation

Work with three real published reviews — name their structure, tell summary from synthesis, find the gap, and score them with a rubric.

📚 The three real reviews you will analyse

All examples below are taken from published papers, each illustrating a different way to organise a literature review. The full PDFs are on your Resources page — open them alongside this workshop.

Three ways to organise a review: Thematic — grouped by topic/concept. Chronological — traced over time. Comparative — structured around contrasting positions or groups. Read each real abstract and name its structure.
Summary reports one source at a time ("Author A found X. Author B found Y."). Synthesis connects sources around an idea ("Several studies converge on X (A; B), though they differ on Y."). A literature review must synthesise, not list.
The gap is the sentence where authors say what is missing or under-studied — the opening their study will fill. It often starts with "However," "Despite," "Little research has…," or "limited attention has been paid to…". Find it in each real abstract.
Evaluate a review like a marker. Score each criterion 0–2 (0 = absent, 1 = partial, 2 = strong) for a review you are reading — your group's draft, or one of the samples. Your total appears at the bottom with guidance.
