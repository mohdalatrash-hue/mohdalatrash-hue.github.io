Dialogue & Dialect

Children's books live in their voices — toddler lisp to teen slang. Render speech that is convincing, age-right, and won't date. Based on Lathey (2016), Ch. 4.

💬 Why dialogue is the hard part

Lathey: dialogue drives character and plot and gives young readers respite from description. Hirano notes conversation often needs more ingenuity than descriptive passages. You must build a convincing young vernacular in Arabic — and avoid slang that will sound dated by next year.

📖
Required reading for this workshop: “Dialogue and Dialect in Children’s Literature.” The exercises below practise child speech, slang and politeness from this reading.
The range of child voices: a toddler's lisping inaccuracies, a schoolchild's chatter, a teenager's fashionable slang, regional/urban dialect, and migrant children's heritage-language accents. Each needs a different Arabic solution.
Slang dates fast. Very trendy slang locks a translation to one moment. Aim for a lively but durable young register, and handle politeness/familiarity (which Arabic marks differently from English) with care.
Translate the talk. Give each child a believable Arabic voice — spoken, age-appropriate, in character. Type your version, then reveal the notes.
📝 Review quiz — grounded in “Dialogue and Dialect in Children’s Literature”. 10 questions; each is tagged with the topic it tests (Dialogue craft · Child speech · Slang & dialect · Arabic register). Answer order is shuffled on every attempt.
