Translating for the Stage

On stage, the line must be sayable. Learn where translation ends and adaptation begins, then make real dramatic lines performable in Arabic.

🎭 Speakability comes first

Landers: the essence of theatrical translation is speakability. Meaning, fidelity and precision are secondary to whether an actor can deliver the line naturally. An off-register or tongue-twisting line breaks "the illusion of the first time."

📖
Required reading for this workshop: Landers, “Translating for the Theatre” and “Adaptation or Translation.” The exercises below practise speakability and the translation–adaptation choice from these readings.
The test: say it out loud. If an actor would stumble, re-write — even at some cost to literal precision. Watch for tongue-twisters, unnatural word order, and register that clashes with the character.
Bentley's four "phenomena" (what gets called 'translation'):
From closest to freest
1
Literal / "Pidgin" — so meanly literal the English is barely English. Not performable.
2
Faithful translation — correct, cogent target language, as close to the original as possible. Only this can honestly be called a translation.
3
Adaptation — based on (2) but takes liberties: cuts, interpolations, shifts of style or tone.
4
Variation — a near-original play merely "based on" the foreign one.
After Eric Bentley, via Landers, Literary Translation.
