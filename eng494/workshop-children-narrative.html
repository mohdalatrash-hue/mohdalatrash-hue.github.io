🗣️ Telling a story TO a child

Lathey: writing (and translating) for children is "adult-to-child communication." Oittinen calls it an imaginary dialogue with the child reader — the translator must reach the child of the target culture. Your first job is to preserve that relationship, not just the words.

📖
Required reading for this workshop: Lathey (2016), “Narrative in Children’s Literature” (Ch. 1). Everything below practises the narrator’s voice and address to the child reader.
The storytelling voice is tuned to a young reader — its warmth, simplicity, humour and rhythm are part of the meaning. Flatten it into neutral adult prose and the book stops speaking to children.
Barbara Wall (1991) distinguished how narrators address the reader: single address (speaking to the child alone), double address (winking over the child's head to adults), and the older distant, didactic narrator who lectures or "civilizes." The translator must keep the same stance.
Translate the voice, not just the sense. Keep the narrator's relationship with the child — the direct address, the warmth, the rhythm. Type your version, then reveal the notes.
