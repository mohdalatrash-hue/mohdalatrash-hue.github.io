Unit 1 · Adult Prose

Dialect, Tone & Register

Hear the voice before you translate it — identify register and dialect, choose a strategy, then translate real vernacular excerpts into Arabic.

🎙️ Why this matters

Landers argues that nearly every word sits in a register (formal/informal, technical, regional, vulgar/polite) and that dialect is among the translator's hardest problems. You cannot reproduce English phonetic spelling in Arabic — so you must compensate. This workshop trains the ear, then your pen.

📖
Required reading for this workshop: this workshop covers four Unit 1 readings — Landers, “Style in Translation,” “Register and Tone,” “Dialects,” and “Fiction and Footnotes.” Each is mapped to its own tab below (📘 Style · 🎚️ Register · 🗣️ Dialect · 📕 Footnotes).
📖 Required reading → Landers, “Style in Translation.” Style is the author’s verbal fingerprint — diction, rhythm, tone. Your task is to carry that voice into Arabic, not to “improve” it. The questions below are directly on this reading.
📖 Required reading → Landers, “Register and Tone.” Register is a socially-defined variety of language: formal/informal, technical/non-technical, standard/regional, jargon, and proper/vulgar. The first job is to name the register you hear.
📖 Required reading → Landers, “Dialects.” Dialect signals region, class, era and character. Authors mark it with phonetic spelling and slang. Identify the variety — and the specific feature that will fight you in Arabic.
Now translate. Render each short excerpt into natural Arabic. Type your version, then reveal the translator's notes and a self-assessment checklist. There is no single right answer — judge yours against the criteria.
📖 Required reading → Landers, “Fiction and Footnotes.” Four moves for hard spots: a footnote explains (last resort); an interpolation slips a brief gloss into the text; an omission drops the genuinely untranslatable; compensation recreates the effect elsewhere (e.g. a colloquial Arabic register for English dialect). The first questions below are directly on this reading.
📝 Unit 1 review quiz — all four readings. 24 questions, balanced across Style, Register & Tone, Dialect, and Fiction & Footnotes (6 each). Each question is tagged with the reading it tests; answer order is shuffled on every attempt.
