Unit 4 · Children's Poetry & Song

Singability & Sound

Children's verse is made to be chanted and sung. Keep the beat, the rhyme and the play — then translate a rhyme and a limerick.

🎶 Translate for the tongue and the ear

A nursery rhyme that doesn't bounce, or a song you can't sing to the tune, has failed — however accurate. Here the rhythm, rhyme and sound-play matter as much as the words.

📖
Required reading for this workshop: “Translating Sounds and Reading Aloud.” The exercises below practise singability, metre and sound-play from this reading.
Singability means the words fit a rhythm (and often a known tune). For a song, your Arabic must keep roughly the same number of beats per line so it can still be sung. Test it: clap the beat, or sing it.
The limerick is five lines rhyming AABBA, with a galloping rhythm (long–long–short–short–long). The humour lives in the bounce and the surprise last line. Keep the shape, not every word.
Sound devices — alliteration (repeated first sounds), assonance (repeated vowels), repetition and onomatopoeia — are half the fun of children's verse. Recreate the effect in Arabic, even if the literal words shift.
Now translate. Keep these singable and bouncy. Type your version, sing or clap it, then reveal the notes.
