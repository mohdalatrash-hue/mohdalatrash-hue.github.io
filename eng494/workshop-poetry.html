Unit 3 · Poetry

Translating Poetry: Six Strategies

Every poetry translation sacrifices something. Learn the six strategies, read the rhyme, then translate real verse — twice.

🎵 The translator's dilemma

You usually cannot keep sound, meter, rhyme and sense all at once. Lefevere's six strategies are six different bargains about what to save and what to surrender. Naming your strategy is the first act of translating a poem.

📖
Required reading for this workshop: Landers, “Some Notes on Translating Poetry” and the Lahlali & Abu Hatab lesson. The exercises below practise the six strategies and rhyme/metre choices from these readings.
The six strategies: Phonemic (recreate the sound), Literal (word-for-word sense), Metrical (keep the meter), Verse-to-prose (drop the lines, keep sense), Rhymed (keep the rhyme scheme), Free verse (natural, no meter/rhyme constraint).
Read the form first. Label the rhyme scheme by giving each new end-sound a letter (ABAB, AABB, ABCB…). The scheme is part of what a 'rhymed' or 'metrical' translation must carry.
Translate twice. For each stanza, first make a literal version (sense only), then a second version using the assigned strategy. Reveal the notes to see the trade-offs.
📝 Review quiz — grounded in Landers, “Some Notes on Translating Poetry” & Lahlali & Abu Hatab (Lesson 12). 12 questions; each is tagged with the topic it tests (Landers · Lahlali & Abu Hatab). Answer order is shuffled on every attempt.
