🎼 What is a rhyme scheme?

A rhyme scheme is simply the pattern of end-sounds in a poem, written out as letters. It answers one question, line by line: does this line's ending sound like an earlier one, or is it new?

How to label a rhyme scheme in four steps:
  1. Read the poem aloud — rhyme is a sound pattern, not a spelling pattern.
  2. Give the first line's end-sound the letter A.
  3. If the next line's end-sound is new, give it the next letter (B, then C…).
  4. If a line's end-sound matches an earlier one, reuse that earlier letter.

Five patterns you'll meet again and again:

Couplet
AABB
Rhymes in consecutive pairs — lines 1–2 rhyme, then 3–4 rhyme.
Alternate / cross
ABAB
Every other line rhymes — lines 1–3 rhyme, 2–4 rhyme.
Enclosed
ABBA
The outer lines rhyme, and a separate rhyme is "enclosed" inside them.
Chain (terza rima)
ABA BCB CDC
The middle sound of one stanza becomes the outer sound of the next.
Limerick
AABBA
Lines 1, 2, 5 share one rhyme; the short lines 3–4 share another.

📖 Major example — “The Road Not Taken”

Robert Frost's most famous poem is built from four five-line stanzas, and — quietly, without ever forcing it — every stanza uses the same rhyme scheme. Read it once for the story; then look at the letters.

The Road Not TakenRobert Frost (1916) · public domain
ATwo roads diverged in a yellow wood,
BAnd sorry I could not travel both
AAnd be one traveler, long I stood
AAnd looked down one as far as I could
BTo where it bent in the undergrowth;
Stanza 1 — ABAAB (wood / stood / could share A; both / undergrowth share B)
AThen took the other, as just as fair,
BAnd having perhaps the better claim,
ABecause it was grassy and wanted wear;
AThough as for that the passing there
BHad worn them really about the same,
Stanza 2 — ABAAB again
AAnd both that morning equally lay
BIn leaves no step had trodden black.
AOh, I kept the first for another day!
AYet knowing how way leads on to way,
BI doubted if I should ever come back.
Stanza 3 — ABAAB again
AI shall be telling this with a sigh
BSomewhere ages and ages hence:
ATwo roads diverged in a wood, and I—
AI took the one less traveled by,
BAnd that has made all the difference.
Stanza 4 — ABAAB, unchanged to the last line
Why this matters for translation: Frost holds ABAAB steady across all sixteen lines without it ever sounding forced — that discipline is part of the poem's quiet, deliberate voice. A translator choosing a rhymed strategy for this poem isn't just matching sounds; they're trying to protect that same quiet control.

🔊 From reading rhyme to translating sound: phonemic translation

Today's reading, the Lahlali & Abu Hatab lesson, opens with Bassnett-McGuire's six strategies for translating poetry. The first of the six is the one we practice today.

Phonemic translation
“Attempts to recreate the sounds of the SL [source language] in the TL [target language], transferring the meaning at the same time.”
Bassnett-McGuire (1980), as cited in Lahlali & Abu Hatab (2014, p. 44).
Reference (APA 7th ed.): Lahlali, E. M., & Abu Hatab, W. (2014). Advanced English–Arabic translation: A practical guide. Edinburgh University Press.

In plain terms: you are not first asking “what does this line mean?” — you are asking “what does this line sound like?” and hunting for Arabic words that make a similar sound, while still keeping the sense in view. It is the hardest of the six strategies to sustain over a whole poem, which is exactly why we only practice it one stanza at a time.

How to attempt a phonemic translation:
  1. Read the English stanza aloud. Which sounds repeat — a hard consonant, a long vowel, an end-rhyme?
  2. Underline the words carrying that sound.
  3. Brainstorm Arabic words that are close in meaning and also echo that sound (alliteration, assonance, or a matching end-rhyme).
  4. Accept that meaning will bend slightly — that trade-off is the whole point of this strategy.

👂 Watch one done first — a worked phonemic attempt

Spotting a rhyme scheme is a reading skill; a phonemic translation is a making skill — and the jump between them is real. So before you try your own, here is the whole four-step method walked through once, out loud, on the stanza we already know best.

The Road Not Taken — stanza 1Robert Frost (1916) · scheme ABAAB
Two roads diverged in a yellow wood, And sorry I could not travel both And be one traveler, long I stood And looked down one as far as I could To where it bent in the undergrowth;— the A-sound to chase: the held, rounded -ood in wood / stood / could
Step 1 — hear it. Read aloud. Three line-endings land on the same deep, rounded vowel: wood, stood, could. That held /uː/ is the sound doing the work.
Step 2 — underline it. Mark wood / stood / could (the three A lines), and separately both / undergrowth (the two B lines).
Step 3 — hunt an Arabic sound. Look for Arabic line-endings that ring on a long و and stay near the meaning — endings like ـود / ـوت / ـول for the A lines, and a second, different sound for the two B lines.
Step 4 — accept the bend. To keep three endings ringing on that و, an image or two will shift a little. That small surrender is the strategy: you are buying sound with a little sense.

A sample direction — not a finished translation:

اجعلْ نهايات الأسطر ١ و٣ و٤ (سطور الـA) تتردّد على مدٍّ طويل بالواو — مثل: يعود، الوجود، أعود — ثم اختر صوتًا مختلفًا يربط السطرين ٢ و٥ (سطور الـB)، كنهايةٍ بالباء: الدروب / الغروب.

See the trade-off in action: to keep three endings ringing on that long و, the exact images (which road, the undergrowth) shift a little in the wording. A different translator would bend different words — there is no single right answer, only a defensible bargain.

🧭 A translator's question — whose sound sets the terms?

Phonemic translation quietly asks Arabic to chase an English sound, and that is worth pausing on. English and Arabic do not meet as equals in the global traffic of translation: English is far more often the prestige source and Arabic the receiving language, so bending Arabic to echo English can mean muting Arabic's own music — its root patterns, its عَروض, its own rhymes — to serve someone else's. Notice too that three of today's poems are nature poems (Frost's woods and falling snow, Dickinson's bird): chasing their sound also carries a very English picture of landscape into an Arabic ear. None of this means “don't do it.” It means that when you choose to chase a sound, it is worth knowing whose sound you are chasing, and what you are choosing to quiet in order to get it.
